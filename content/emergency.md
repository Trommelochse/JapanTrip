## 1. The numbers to memorize

- **110** — Police.
- **119** — Fire **and** ambulance. Same number for both.
- **118** — Coast guard / sea emergencies. Not relevant for this route.

All three are free from any phone, no SIM required. English operators exist but are not guaranteed instantly — speak slowly, say your location first ("kyukyusha onegaishimasu" = "ambulance please"). Hotel staff calling for you is almost always faster than dialing yourself.

**Non-emergency, English-speaking support lines:**
- **JNTO Visitor Hotline — 050-3816-2787** · 24/7 · English, Chinese, Korean. The single best number to call if you're sick, lost, or stuck and unsure what to do.
- **AMDA International Medical Information Center — 03-6233-9266** · Mon–Fri 10:00–15:00 · finds you an English-speaking doctor by phone.
- **TELL Lifeline — 03-5774-0992** · daily 09:00–23:00 · English mental-health / crisis line. Not medical.

## 2. English-speaking medical care, by city

### Tokyo

- **St. Luke's International Hospital (Tsukiji)** — 9-1 Akashi-cho, Chuo-ku · **03-3541-5151** · 24h ER · English-speaking staff in most departments · accepts foreign cards. The default for anything serious in Tokyo. 7 min walk from Tsukiji station.
- **Tokyo Medical & Surgical Clinic** — 32 Shiba-koen Bldg 2F, 3-4-30 Shiba-koen, Minato-ku · **03-3436-3028** · Mon–Fri walk-ins, by appointment otherwise · self-pay only (no Japanese insurance, but foreign cards fine) · founded 1951, the long-running expat GP. Best for non-emergency stuff — UTIs, sprains, a cold that won't quit.
- **Sanno Hospital** — 8-10-16 Akasaka, Minato-ku · **03-3402-3151** · reception Mon–Sat 08:30–11:30, 13:00–16:30 · most doctors speak English · accepts cards. Good mid-option, walking distance from Aoyama-itchome.
- **Tokyo Midtown Clinic (Roppongi)** — directly connected to Roppongi station · English-speaking staff · cards accepted. Useful if you're already in that part of the city.

### Hakone (Days 6–7, Oct 28–29)

Rural. There is no English-speaking clinic in Hakone itself. Realistically:
- **Minor stuff** (cut, headache, indigestion): ask the ryokan. They will produce a first-aid kit and, if needed, a thermometer and a recommendation.
- **Serious**: ryokan calls 119, you go to **Odawara** (25–40 min by ambulance). The closest equipped hospital is **Odawara Municipal Hospital** (Kuno 46, Odawara · 0465-34-3175 · 24h ER). English support is limited — bring Google Translate.
- **Odawara Hakone Medical Center** (next to Odawara station) has multilingual info reports (EN/ZH/VI) but is geared to checkups, not emergencies.

### Takayama (Days 8–10, Oct 30 – Nov 1)

- **Japanese Red Cross Takayama Hospital** — 3-11 Tenmachi, Takayama, Gifu 506-8550 · **0577-32-1111** · 24h ER · the only real option for anything beyond a clinic visit. **No staff translators** — bring your phone with Google Translate. Some doctors do speak some English; do not count on it.
- For anything minor, ask your ryokan/hotel — they can call ahead to a local clinic and confirm a doctor who can manage in English or via translator.

### Kyoto (Days 11–14, Nov 2–5)

- **Japan Baptist Hospital** — 47 Kitashirakawa Yamanomotocho, Sakyo-ku · **075-781-5191** · English-speaking doctors and nurses on staff · cards accepted. This is the default for foreigners in Kyoto. Northeast Kyoto, ~20 min by taxi from central Higashiyama.
- **Kyoto University Hospital** — 54 Kawahara-cho, Shogoin, Sakyo-ku · **075-751-3111** · 24h ER · advanced treatment, multilingual signage on the ground floor, English varies by department. Use for serious cases.
- For Day 15 (KIX departure morning), you'll be in transit — if anything happens between Kyoto and the airport, JNTO Visitor Hotline (050-3816-2787) is the fastest triage.

## 3. Pharmacies

The big chains, all easy to spot:
- **Matsumoto Kiyoshi** (yellow + black) — densest, longest hours.
- **Sundrug**, **Welcia**, **Kokumin**, **OS Drug** — interchangeable.

Most pharmacy staff do **not** speak English. Use Google Translate camera mode on the box.

**What's OTC in Japan that's prescription elsewhere:** very little. The flow goes the other way.

**What's restricted / banned that Westerners often carry:**
- **Pseudoephedrine** (Sudafed, Vicks Inhaler, many Western cold meds): banned. Anything over 10% pseudoephedrine falls under the Stimulants Control Law and **cannot be brought in even with a prescription.** If you have Sudafed in your bag, leave it in Tallinn.
- **Codeine**-containing painkillers: restricted, max 30-day supply for personal use.
- **ADHD stimulants** (Adderall, Vyvanse, methylphenidate): banned for import. Strattera (atomoxetine) is fine.
- **Yakkan Shoumei** (import certificate): needed if you're bringing more than a 1-month supply of OTC, more than 24 doses of injectable, or any controlled substance. Apply via the Kanto-Shinetsu MHLW bureau, ideally 2–4 weeks before travel. **Neither of you carries anything that requires this** based on the briefing, but flag it if that changes.

## 4. What you can actually buy at the drugstore

For the things that come up on a 14-day trip:

- **Hydration / "I walked 22km today" recovery**: Pocari Sweat or Aquarius. Available everywhere, conbini and pharmacies both.
- **Cold / flu**: **Pabron Gold A** (Taisho) is the standard Japanese cold-medicine, sold everywhere. **Lulu** is the alternative. Both target cough + sore throat + fever.
- **Stomach upset / overate kaiseki**: **Cabagin Kowa** (cabbage-extract antacid, classic) or **Ohta's Isan** (the powder in the green tin — works fast).
- **GI / mild food poisoning**: **Seirogan** (the black pills, smells terrible, works) or activated charcoal tablets.
- **Painkillers**: **Loxonin S** (loxoprofen, prescription-strength NSAID, sold OTC) is the strongest you can get without a doctor. **Eve** is the ibuprofen equivalent. Tylenol/acetaminophen sold as **Tylenol A** or **Bufferin Luna**.
- **Blister / band-aids**: **Band-Aid Kizu Power Pad** — the hydrocolloid ones, very good for foot blisters from temple walking.
- **Masks**: every conbini, every pharmacy. Useful if either of you picks up a cold mid-trip — it's a courtesy in Japan and nobody will stare.

## 5. Lost passport — step by step (German citizens)

**Step 1: File a police report.** Go to the nearest **koban** (small police box, on most major intersections). Ask for a "lost item report" (遺失届, *ishitsu-todoke*) if it was lost, or "theft report" (盗難届, *tounan-todoke*) if stolen. They will give you a paper receipt. Keep it — the embassy requires it.

**Step 2: Call the German diplomatic mission.**

- **German Embassy Tokyo** — 4-5-10 Minami-Azabu, Minato-ku, Tokyo 106-0047 · **+81-3-5791-7700** · Mon–Fri 08:00–12:00 · email information@tokyo.diplo.de
- **German Consulate General Osaka** — Umeda Sky Building, Tower East, 35F, 1-1-88-3501 Oyodonaka, Kita-ku, Osaka 531-6035 · **+81-6-6440-5070** · by appointment only.

Tokyo covers the Tokyo / Hakone / Takayama legs. Osaka covers the Kyoto / KIX leg — **if the passport disappears Nov 2 or later, go to Osaka, not Tokyo.** It's an hour from Kyoto by Shinkansen vs. 3+ from Hakone direction.

**After-hours / weekend emergencies**: call the embassy main line; it routes to a duty officer.

**Step 3: Bring to the appointment:**
- Police report receipt
- 1–2 biometric passport photos (DM, Bic Camera, station photo booths all have machines that produce compliant prints)
- Any other German ID (Personalausweis is gold here; driver's license helps)
- Flight booking, accommodation address
- Cash or card for the fee (emergency passport / *Reisepass als Notpass*: around €37 for the temporary, €81+ for a full one)

**Realistic timeline**: Notpass (temporary, valid 1 year, machine-readable but no chip — accepted for return to Schengen) issued in **24–48 hours** if all the documents are clean. A full ePassport takes 4–6 weeks and gets shipped to your home address — not relevant for this trip.

## 6. Travel insurance

The **German/EU EHIC does not work in Japan.** Japan has no bilateral healthcare agreement with the EU. Every yen is out-of-pocket at point of service unless you have private travel insurance.

What to look for in your policy:
- **Medical evacuation: €100,000 minimum.** This is the big one. A medevac flight Tokyo–Frankfurt with a medical escort is €60–150k. Without coverage you are personally on the hook.
- **In-country medical: €30,000+** covers almost any non-evac scenario.
- **24h emergency claims line** that you can call from Japan. **Write this number on a card and put it in your wallet** — not just on your phone. Phones break, get stolen, or run out of battery in the worst moments.
- **Direct billing to Japanese hospitals** is rare; expect to pay upfront and reclaim. Carry a credit card with enough headroom for a €5–10k charge.

No specific provider recommendation here — German market choices (HanseMerkur, ERGO Reiseversicherung, ADAC) all do this fine. Just verify the evac number.

## 7. Weather risks

- **Typhoon tail risk: Hakone, Oct 28–29.** Late October is the back edge of the western Pacific typhoon season. A direct hit at this date is unlikely but tail rain can shut down the Hakone ropeway and Owakudani. **Check [jma.go.jp/en](https://www.jma.go.jp/jma/indexe.html) on the morning of Day 6 before leaving Tokyo.** Romancecar runs in most weather; the ropeway does not.
- **Earthquakes.** Japan has minor tremors weekly; most are nothing. If a real one hits:
  - **Do NOT run outside.** Falling debris is what injures people.
  - Get under a sturdy table or against an interior wall, away from windows and tall furniture.
  - In a ryokan: tatami room, get low, cover your head. The buildings are engineered for this.
  - When it stops, open a door (so it doesn't jam from frame deformation) and wait. NHK World will push alerts within 30 seconds.
- **Heat is not a factor** at this time of year. October–November Honshu is 10–20°C. Cold mornings in Takayama/Shirakawa-go can hit near-freezing — pack layers, not heat-stroke kit.

## 8. Personal safety

Japan is one of the safest countries on Earth. You can walk anywhere in Tokyo or Kyoto at 2am. Say so plainly — no fearmongering needed.

Two real flags:

- **Gion private alleys, Kyoto.** Since April 2024, tourists are banned from the small private side-alleys off Hanamikoji in Gion. **Fine: ¥10,000.** The main Hanamikoji street and public areas are fine. Multilingual signs mark the off-limits alleys — read them. This is on Day 13's evening walk.
- **Bag-of-opportunity theft** in crowded zones: Shinjuku station and bar district (Friday/Saturday late), Shibuya Crossing, Nishiki Market in Kyoto, Shijo-Kawaramachi at peak hours. Not pickpocketing in the European sense — more "phone left on a café table for 20 minutes." Standard wallet-in-front-pocket sense applies.

## 9. Apps to install before you fly

Download in Tallinn, on hotel wifi — not on Japanese roaming.

- **Google Translate** + **pre-download the Japanese offline pack** + **enable camera mode**. The single most important app. Reads menus, medicine boxes, train signs.
- **Google Maps**. Works flawlessly in Japan, including train timetables, platform numbers, and walking transfers inside stations.
- **NHK World**. English news + earthquake / tsunami / typhoon push alerts. Install and enable notifications.
- **LINE**. Japan's WhatsApp. Useful if you meet anyone locally or book any service that uses it (some ryokan front desks do).
- **Japan Official Travel App** (JNTO). Has the visitor hotline pre-dialed and offline emergency-phrase support.

Skip every other "must-have Japan app" listicle. The five above cover communication and safety; the rest is noise.
