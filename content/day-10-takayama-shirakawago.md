# Day 10 — Sunday, Nov 1 2026 — Shirakawa-go day trip

> **Theme:** The foliage payoff of the trip. Beat the Sunday day-tripper wave by an hour, photograph from the Shiroyama viewpoint before tour buses arrive, then wander Ogimachi village at human pace.
> **Weather (typical):** 2–13°C in the valley, frost likely at dawn, dry. Shirakawa-go is a snow valley — by Nov 1 it's not snowy yet but it *is* properly cold. Hat, gloves, base layer, a real jacket. The morning bus runs cold.
> **Walking:** Medium (≈7–9 km, one moderate hill up to the viewpoint or shuttle bus alternative).

## Schedule

### Morning
- **06:15** — Wake. Ryokan breakfast is usually too late — ask the night before for a **bento breakfast** (most Takayama ryokans will do this for foliage-season Shirakawa-go guests with a day's notice; ~¥1,000 supplement). Otherwise, grab onigiri + hot canned coffee from the **FamilyMart at Takayama Station's east exit** (24h).
- **07:15** — Walk to **Takayama Nōhi Bus Center** (right next to JR Takayama Station). Pick up tickets at the counter (purchased online — see practical file). Bag stowage in the hold is free.
- **07:50** — **Nōhi Bus to Shirakawa-go** · **50 minutes** · ¥2,800 one-way (¥5,000 round-trip if booked together). **This is the second bus of the day; the 7:20 sells out for foliage Sundays 2–3 weeks ahead, and arrives before the village's parking lot opens. The 7:50 is the sweet spot: arrives 08:40, ahead of the 9:00–10:30 wave from Kanazawa and the Nagoya tour buses.** Reservation **required** for foliage-season departures — book online via Nohi Bus or Japan Bus Online **as soon as the 1-month window opens (Oct 1)** for Sunday Nov 1.
- **08:40** — Arrive **Shirakawa-go bus terminal**. Coin lockers exist but are limited; if you packed lean, just keep your daypack. Toilets here are good — use them now.
- **08:50** — **Walk up to Shiroyama Viewpoint (Ogimachi Castle Ruins Observatory).** Two options:
  - **(A) Walk up the path** (15–20 min, moderate, paved switchbacks through forest, foliage corridor on the way) — **do this**. The path entrance is a 5-min walk north of the bus terminal, past the Deai Bridge.
  - **(B) Shuttle bus** (¥200, runs 9:00–15:40, ~every 20 min) — boring, and you'll wait. Only relevant on the way down if knees complain.
- **09:15** — **Shiroyama Viewpoint** · free · 30–45 min. The iconic Shirakawa-go shot — the cluster of gasshō-zukuri farmhouses framed by red maples, rice paddies just harvested, the Shokawa river snaking through, snow-dusted peaks behind. **By 09:30 the first wave of day-trippers starts arriving. By 10:30 the railing is two people deep.** You'll have a good 30 minutes of relative calm if you stick to the 7:50 bus plan. The small kiosk at the top sells hot drinks; the toilet is decent.

### Afternoon
- **10:00** — Walk back down via the Deai Bridge (suspension footbridge across the Shokawa — slightly bouncy, photogenic from the riverbank).
- **10:30–13:30** — **Wander block: Ogimachi village.** The village itself is the wander. ~110 gasshō-zukuri houses, of which 1/3 are still lived in (respect the *no entry* signs and don't photograph through windows). Pick a few open-to-public houses to enter:
  - **Wada House** (Wada-ke, ¥400, 9:00–17:00) — the largest and grandest, former village headman, original 1850s ledgers. Allow 30 min.
  - **Kanda House** (¥400, 9:00–17:00) — smaller, attic level walkable, you can see the silk-worm racks. Allow 20 min.
  - **Myōzen-ji Temple Museum** (¥400, 9:00–17:00) — temple plus the priest's living quarters in gasshō style. Skip if Wada + Kanda feel like enough.
  - **Avoid** the Gasshō-zukuri Folk Park across the river — it's a relocated-houses outdoor museum like Hida-no-Sato but smaller and slicker. We did the better version yesterday.
- **12:30** — Lunch: **Irori** · 374 Ogimachi · soba and Hida beef in a 200-year-old gasshō house with an actual sunken hearth (*irori*) burning in the center of the room. Order the **soba teishoku** with mountain vegetables. Cash only, no reservations, expect a 15–30 min wait at peak — but it's worth it for the architecture alone. 10:00–14:30, closed Wed. **Alternative if Irori is wrecked:** **Shirakawa-go-no-yu** (the public bath complex) has a casual cafeteria with serviceable Hida beef curry; or grab a **gohei-mochi** (skewered grilled rice with walnut-miso glaze) from one of the village stalls and call it a moving lunch.
- **14:00** — Continue wandering the back lanes — the eastern side of the village (away from the main parking lot) is markedly quieter. Walk to the small **Akiba-jinja shrine** on the northeast edge for a different angle on the rooftops.
- **15:00** — Coffee + warm-up: **Falò Coffee Roaster Shirakawa-go branch** (if open — verify; the Takayama parent shop opened a small Shirakawa-go outpost mid-2024) OR **Cafe Kiraku** for sweet bean *zenzai* and matcha in a thatched-roof teahouse setting.
- **15:45** — Walk back to the bus terminal. Last bus options below.

### Evening
- **16:10** or **17:10** — **Nōhi Bus Shirakawa-go → Takayama** · 50 min · reservation strongly recommended for foliage Sunday. **The 17:10 is the last reasonable one — there's a 17:50 in some seasonal schedules but verify on the day; don't gamble on it.**
- **17:00** or **18:00** — Back in Takayama. Ryokan onsen first.
- **19:00** — Dinner: **Kyōya again** if it landed for you on Day 8 (book a different course this time — the *miyabi* kaiseki with Hida beef shabu-shabu, ~¥6,500). **Or** the strong alternative: **Tsuzumi-soba** · 56 Kamininomachi · 70-year-old Takayama-ramen specialty shop, run by the third generation. Chicken-bone shoyu broth, very thin straight noodles — pure, clear, restrained. Order the *chū-ka soba* with extra menma. ¥900. 11:30–14:00 / 17:00–21:00 (or until sold out — and they often sell out by 19:30 on Sundays). Closed Tue, Wed.
- **20:30** — Final Takayama onsen. Tomorrow you leave for Kyoto early-ish.

## Rain plan B
If Nov 1 is genuinely wet (>5mm forecast), Shirakawa-go is still worth doing — the gasshō roofs were *built* for weather and look more dramatic in mist. **But:** the Shiroyama viewpoint is the loss; in heavy rain or fog you see nothing. In that case, swap the viewpoint for a longer Wada House + Myōzen-ji visit, take the shuttle up only if the sky clears at 11–12, and accept the trip as an architecture-and-village day rather than a photography day. Rain pants > umbrella here.

## Photography golden hour
- **Sunrise (~6:20):** Not in Shirakawa-go (you haven't arrived yet). Skip; the village faces east-southeast and the viewpoint actually shoots better in mid-morning soft light than at literal sunrise.
- **Shiroyama Viewpoint:** **best 09:15–10:00** — sun fully on the village, mist often still drifting off the rice paddies, crowd manageable.
- **Sunset (~17:00 from Takayama):** You'll be on the bus or just back; not a Shirakawa-go shot. Catch the Higashiyama hills from the Miyagawa instead.
- **Specific shot:** Shiroyama viewpoint — wide horizontal frame, lower third river + paddies, middle third village cluster, upper third the autumn ridge. 24–35mm equivalent. The over-photographed shot is the slightly-narrower vertical with one farmhouse anchoring the foreground — both work; the wide is rarer.

## Reservations needed for this day
- **Nōhi Bus Takayama→Shirakawa-go 07:50** — **CRITICAL. Book Oct 1** (the 1-month-out moment) via [nouhibus.co.jp](https://www.nouhibus.co.jp/route_bus/takayama-shirakawago-line-en/) or Japan Bus Online. Sunday + foliage = fills fast.
- **Nōhi Bus return** — book both directions in one round-trip booking; cheaper and locks the return.

## Off-path notes
- **Don't cross to the parking-lot side of the river** unless transiting. The west side (where the buses dump you) has more tourist stalls; the east side (the actual village) is where you want to be.
- The "no drones" rule is enforced — don't even unbox one. Locals will (politely) intervene.
- **Sonja note:** the village has a small **soap workshop** (Shirakawa-go Sekken) near the south end, hand-milled with local hot-spring minerals. Worth a 10-minute stop and a souvenir bar.
- **Clemens note:** if you have any interest in the *gasshō-zukuri* construction technique itself — the rope-tied roof beams, the lack of nails, the steam-bent timbers — the **upper floor of Wada House** is the only place you'll see the structural detail in full. Don't rush it.
- One genuine local lunch alternative that's not in the guidebooks: **Masuen Bunsuke** (北口 farmhouse café on the village edge) does a single set lunch — river fish + mountain veg + brown rice — and seats 12. Verify it's open on Sundays before counting on it (recent listings say yes, but small operations shift).
