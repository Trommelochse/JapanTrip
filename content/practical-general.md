# Practical — General

Cross-cutting stuff that applies across cities. The city-specific files (`practical-tokyo.md` etc.) cover local quirks. This file covers what's true everywhere.

## Language

Japan is more English-friendly than China but less than Hong Kong or Singapore. Train stations, major museums, and tourist-area restaurants have English signage and at least one staffer who'll get you through a transaction. Side-street izakaya, neighborhood ramen shops, and rural Hakone / Takayama ryokans often have no English — and that's fine.

The killer apps:
- **Google Translate** with the Japanese offline pack downloaded (download before leaving Wi-Fi)
- The **camera/photo mode** translates menus, signs, ingredient labels in real time
- **Google Maps** for everything transit-related — flawless in Japan including timetable accuracy and platform numbers
- **LINE** for any local contacts (Japan's WhatsApp equivalent — Western messaging apps are rare)

Phrases worth memorizing (Romaji):
- *Sumimasen* — excuse me / sorry / thank you (the universal one)
- *Arigato gozaimasu* — thank you (formal)
- *Eigo no menyu wa arimasu ka?* — do you have an English menu?
- *Okaikei onegaishimasu* — bill please

Don't bother with "konnichiwa" — staff will greet you with *irasshaimase* and the expected response is silence (not a return greeting).

## Cash vs cards

2024–25 has been the inflection point — cards and IC-card-tap are now accepted at major shops, all hotels, most chain restaurants, train station kiosks, and depachika. But **small restaurants, shrines, temples, ryokan town shops, and rural areas (Takayama, Shirakawa-go, parts of Hakone) are still cash-mostly.**

Working rule:
- **Carry ¥20,000–30,000 in cash per person at any given time.**
- **7-Eleven ATMs** accept Visa / Mastercard / Amex / Maestro / Cirrus, 24h, no fee from the Japanese side. Your home bank may charge ~€3–5 per withdrawal.
- **Lawson and Family Mart ATMs** also accept foreign cards but are less consistently reliable than 7-Eleven.
- **Japan Post ATMs** accept foreign cards but only during post-office hours.

Big-ticket items where cash matters: Saiho-ji entry (¥4,000 cash on the day), most Hakone Free Pass top-ups if buying paper, Nohi Bus to Shirakawa-go (cash on board if QR fails), all the small Sanmachi shops in Takayama, the temple admission fees in Kyoto.

## Suica / Pasmo / ICOCA

One IC card per person, tap-and-go for **every train and bus in Tokyo + Yokohama + Hakone Tozan + Kyoto + Osaka + Kobe + Nagoya** and most regional networks. Plus konbini, vending machines, many small restaurants.

The smart move:
- **Add Suica to iPhone / Apple Watch** via Wallet on the flight in. Works at ~95% of turnstiles. Top up from any credit card in 30 seconds.
- **Android users** can use Mobile Suica via the Suica app, but it's clunkier with foreign cards — easier to grab a physical Welcome Suica at the airport.
- **One card per person** — you can't tap two people through on one card.
- **Load ¥3,000–5,000 to start.** Top up at any station or konbini.

The IC card does NOT replace:
- Shinkansen tickets (separate reservation)
- Hakone Romancecar limited-express seat (separate reservation)
- Hida Wide View limited-express seat (separate reservation)
- Nohi Bus to Shirakawa-go (separate ticket)
- Haruka to KIX limited-express seat (separate reservation)

## eSIM / connectivity

Get a data eSIM before departure. Reliable options:
- **Ubigi** (works on every modern phone, easy installation)
- **Airalo** (huge plan variety, occasional activation issues)
- **IIJmio** (Japan-native, slightly cheaper but harder to set up for non-Japanese accounts)

10–20 GB for 14 days is plenty for two travelers (~€15–25 pp). **Data-only is fine** — you don't need a Japanese phone number. WhatsApp / iMessage / Signal cover messaging.

**Wi-Fi at every hotel and ryokan** including Sumiyoshi and Hakone Suimeisou. Cafes, konbini (7-Eleven, Lawson, Family Mart), and major train stations all have free open Wi-Fi.

## Etiquette quick hits

The ones that actually matter day-to-day:

- **No tipping. Anywhere. Ever.** Including taxis, ryokan staff, kaiseki chef. A short bow and *arigato gozaimasu* is the correct gesture.
- **Remove shoes** when entering homes, ryokans (at the front entrance), some restaurants (look for the raised floor and slipper basket), most temple buildings, and changing rooms.
- **No phone calls on trains.** Texting fine. Speaker phone never.
- **No eating while walking** — exception: designated food streets (Yanaka Ginza, Komachi-dori in Kamakura, Nishiki Market in Kyoto, Sanmachi snack street in Takayama).
- **Stand on the left** on escalators in Tokyo (right is for walkers). Reversed in Osaka — but you're not in Osaka long enough for it to matter.
- **Bow slightly** when thanking, when entering shops, when receiving something with two hands. Don't overdo it.
- **Don't blow your nose at the table.** Excuse yourself.
- **Slurping noodles is fine.** Encouraged, even.
- **Onsen rules:** wash and rinse fully at the seated showers BEFORE entering the bath. No swimsuits. No towel in the water. Tie long hair up. No phones in any onsen area, ever. Most splurge ryokans accommodate tattoos quietly (Hakone Suimeisou: ask at check-in; small tattoos are usually fine).

## Weather window (Oct 23 – Nov 6)

Average temperature ranges for your dates:

| City | Day high | Night low | Rain prob |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tokyo (Oct 23–27) | 17–19°C | 10–13°C | ~25%, mostly light |
| Hakone (Oct 28–29) | 14–17°C | 9–11°C | ~30%, **late-October typhoon-tail risk** |
| Takayama (Oct 30–Nov 1) | 12–15°C | 4–7°C; **2°C in Shirakawa-go valley at dawn** | Dry (alpine sheltered from coastal weather) |
| Kyoto (Nov 2–6) | 16–18°C | 9–11°C | ~20%, mostly light |

**The packing strategy** is layering: base layer + mid layer (fleece or light down) + light rain shell. One pair of comfortable walking shoes (Allbirds, Hokas, or equivalent). See [packing.md](packing.md) for the full list.

**The typhoon contingency** for Hakone is real. Check the Japan Meteorological Agency forecast (jma.go.jp) the morning of Day 6. If a typhoon is tracking in, the ropeway closes; see [day-07-hakone.md](day-07-hakone.md) for the rain plan B.

## Currency math

- **1 EUR ≈ ¥165** as of May 2026 (verify at trip time; the yen has been volatile)
- **1 USD ≈ ¥153**
- Quick mental conversion: divide yen prices by 1,000 then add ~10% to get rough EUR. ¥3,000 ≈ €18. ¥10,000 ≈ €60. ¥48,000 (a typical mid-tier ryokan night for two) ≈ €290.

## Trash + recycling

Notable Japanese quirk: **almost no public trash bins anywhere.** Carry your waste back to a konbini, your hotel, or a train station (some stations have bins near the ticket gates). This is universal — don't be surprised.

## Power

- **Type A plug** (same as US — two flat pins). Bring an adapter from the European Type C/F.
- **100V at 50Hz (east) / 60Hz (west).** Modern electronics (laptops, phones, camera chargers) handle both. Hair dryers and curling irons from Europe will burn out; every hotel provides one.

## Connectivity-free zones to know about

- **Shiroyama viewpoint Shirakawa-go** can get spotty at peak congestion
- **Inside some Kyoto temples** (especially Saiho-ji, where you don't have your phone out anyway)
- **The Hida Wide View train** through the deep gorges — short patches of no signal, plan downloads accordingly
- **Mountain temple grounds** (e.g., upper Higashiyama trails behind Shiroyama Park, Takayama) can drop service
