# Packing — Japan, Oct 23 – Nov 6, 2026

Two people, 14 nights, Tokyo → Hakone → Takayama → Kyoto. Mild city to cold mountain village and back. Two takkyubin forwards in the middle. This list is tuned to *this* trip, not generic Japan advice.

## The packing strategy

Layering is the whole game: a thin merino base, a mid-layer fleece or light down, and a packable rain shell handle 6°C frosty Shirakawa-go dawn (Day 10) just as well as 18°C Tokyo afternoons (Days 1–5). Bags split into three units: one **big suitcase per person** that gets takkyubin'd Tokyo → Takayama on Day 6 (see practical-tokyo.md) and Takayama → Kyoto on Day 11 (see practical-takayama.md), one **shared small overnight bag** for the 48-hour Hakone window, and one **daypack per person** that never leaves your side. Target one suitcase + one daypack per person — you'll be lugging carry-ons on the Romancecar (Day 6) and the cramped Hida #36 (Day 11), so anything that doesn't fit those two pieces stays home. Leave ~20% bag volume free on departure for Sonja's J-beauty haul.

## Big suitcase (per person, gets takkyubin'd)

Clothing and bulk toiletries. Reappears in Takayama on the evening of Day 8.

- 6–7 merino/synthetic t-shirts or long-sleeve base layers (no cotton — slow to dry, cold when damp)
- 2 button-down or smart-casual overshirts (kaiseki dinner is in-room yukata, but evenings out in Kyoto warrant nicer than a t-shirt)
- 1 light down jacket (Uniqlo Ultra Light Down or Patagonia Down Sweater class — packs to a fist, handles 2°C Shirakawa-go dawn under a shell, layers off entirely in Tokyo)
- 1 mid-weight fleece or merino sweater (the in-between layer for 10–14°C city days)
- 2 pairs trousers (1 dark wool/synthetic blend, 1 lighter chino or technical pant). No jeans — slow to dry, heavy.
- 1 pair thermal leggings/long johns for the Shirakawa-go day
- 7 pairs underwear, 7 pairs socks (5 regular merino + 2 *nice* socks — see ryokan note below)
- Pyjamas / loungewear (the ryokan provides yukata but you'll want something for Kyoto hotels)
- Toiletries: full-size toothpaste, deodorant, shampoo/conditioner, sunscreen SPF 30+ (UV is still real in October), moisturizer, lip balm
- Quick-dry travel towel — small (everywhere provides towels, but useful as a sit-pad / hot-spring head-towel)
- Laundry: 1 mesh bag, a small bottle of detergent sheets (ryokan and most Kyoto hotels have coin laundry)

## Hakone overnight kit (one shared small bag, ~30L cabin size)

Covers Days 6–7 and the **morning of Day 8 in case the forwarded bags are delayed** (see practical-takayama.md — overnight kit doctrine).

- 2 changes of base layer + underwear + socks per person (covers two ryokan nights + a buffer day)
- 1 mid-layer each (the fleece — Owakudani ropeway on Day 7 is exposed and windy)
- 1 packable rain shell each (Hakone is the typhoon-tail risk window — see practical-hakone.md)
- Toiletries dop kit: toothbrush, travel-size toothpaste, contact lens supplies, any daily meds, basic skincare
- Phone charger + 1 short cable each
- Camera + 1 spare battery (Open-Air Museum on Day 7 is photo-heavy)
- ¥20,000 cash per person for Hakone (ryokan settles on card; local lunch spots in Gora often cash-only)
- Reusable water bottle
- The Hakone Free Pass QR (EMot app) and Romancecar seat reservation — both on your phone

## Daypack (always with you, on the plane, on every train)

The "if everything else is lost, we're still fine" bag.

- Passport + photocopies (stored separately) + travel insurance card
- Wallet, IC card (Suica on iPhone Wallet preferred), credit cards, ~¥30,000 starter cash
- Phone, eSIM activated, charger, 10,000 mAh power bank (Japanese trains have outlets but not always)
- All prescription meds in original packaging + printed copy of prescriptions (see medical note)
- Saiho-ji confirmation postcard (the temple's reply — you must have it physically on Day 14, see practical-kyoto.md)
- Printed hotel confirmations + flight info as a paper backup
- Camera + the lens you actually use, spare SD card
- Small umbrella (the Muji-style folding kind; ¥600 at any konbini if you forget)
- Tissues / handkerchief (public bathrooms often have neither; the handkerchief is also for drying hands and a polite cultural touch)
- Reusable shopping bag (konbini bags cost ¥3–5 and Japan has no public bins — carry your wrappers)

## Clothing detail

**Layers.** Base = merino t-shirts and one long-sleeve. Mid = one fleece + one Ultra Light Down. Outer = one packable rain shell. The combination of fleece + down + shell handles the Shirakawa-go Nov 1 dawn (~2°C); strip to base + shell for Tokyo's 18°C afternoons.

**Footwear.** **One** pair of broken-in walking shoes (you'll log 15–20 km on Day 13 alone — Fushimi Inari + Tofuku-ji + Gion) plus **one** pair of slip-on sneakers or loafers for ryokan + temple visits where you'll be removing shoes constantly. Saiho-ji, every ryokan, Hida Folk Village, most kaiseki spots = shoes off. Laces are friction.

**Ryokan socks.** You will be visibly sock-footed in front of attendants and other guests. Pack two pairs of *unembarrassing* socks — solid color, no holes, no hotel-freebie polyester. This is the easiest dignity upgrade you'll make.

**Yukata.** Don't pack one. Every ryokan and most Kyoto machiya hotels provide them in-room.

**Rain.** Folding umbrella + packable rain shell. Hakone Day 7 has the typhoon-tail risk; Kyoto Day 13 (Tofuku-ji) often catches light rain in early November. The shell doubles as wind-breaker on the Owakudani ropeway.

## Tech + power

- Japan = **Type A plug, 100V, 50/60 Hz.** German Type F plugs do not fit. Bring 2 Type A adapters (one per person, leave one in the room for charging while the other moves with you).
- Most modern chargers (phone, laptop, camera) are 100–240V — check the brick. Hair tools usually are not — leave them home.
- eSIM: install before flying. **Ubigi, Airalo, or Sakura Mobile** all fine. 15–20 GB plan covers two phones if you hotspot.
- Camera: 1 body, 1 versatile lens, 2 spare batteries, 2 SD cards. Cold Shirakawa-go mornings eat battery life.
- 10,000 mAh power bank — must be in carry-on, not checked, per IATA.

## Medical / health

The Japanese pharmacy system is friendly but has surprising restrictions. Pack from home:

- **Ibuprofen / naproxen** — Japan sells these but at lower max doses (loxoprofen is the local equivalent, OK but weaker). Bring a full bottle.
- **Pseudoephedrine-based decongestants (Sudafed, Aerinaze) are banned** — do not bring or buy. Use a phenylephrine alternative or a saline spray.
- **Strong NSAIDs and codeine-containing painkillers require a prescription** in Japan; small personal quantities are fine, keep them in original boxes.
- **Motion sickness tablets** for the Hida Wide View train (winding river gorge, 4 hours, Day 8 and Day 11) — Sturgeron or Dramamine, take 30 min before boarding.
- Cold/flu basics: throat lozenges, decongestant nasal spray, paracetamol
- Plasters, blister patches (Compeed — you will need these around Day 10)
- Hand sanitizer (small bottle)
- Sonja's regular contraceptive supply for the full trip + a buffer — **specific brands are not interchangeable in Japan** and importing more than a month's supply technically requires a Yakkan Shoumei import certificate. Pack from home, in original packaging.
- Any allergy meds you rely on — cetirizine and loratadine are available OTC in Japan, but specific brands vary.

## Sonja-specific

- **Leave 20% bag volume empty.** Harajuku @cosme (Day 3), Shibuya Loft, Yojiya in Kyoto, and the Don Quijote stops will fill it. A foldable nylon duffle (Uniqlo or Muji, ~€15) packs flat and gives you a second checked bag on the flight home if needed.
- A small Ziploc / pouch for half-used samples and unsealed cosmetics — Japanese customs to the EU is forgiving but tidy is faster.

## Documents

- **Passports** (German citizens get 90-day visa-free entry — no application needed, just show up; Estonian residents same)
- Travel insurance card + 24h claims phone number, saved offline
- Flight info: outbound + **return KIX departure** confirmation (printed, plus on phone)
- All hotel confirmations — printed *and* screenshot (Japanese front desks occasionally want to see the booking ref, paper is faster than scrolling email on jet lag)
- **Saiho-ji confirmation postcard** — the physical reply from the temple. You must have it on Day 14, no card = turned away. See practical-kyoto.md and the reservations checklist; this needs to be in your hand before you leave Tallinn.
- Driving license / second ID for Sonja as a passport backup
- Two passport-sized photos each (for emergency embassy replacement, paranoid but weightless)
- A pen — for landing card and Yamato takkyubin forms

## What NOT to pack

- **Money belt.** Japan is the safest country you'll ever travel. A wallet in your front pocket is fine.
- **Hairdryer.** Every hotel and ryokan has one, and your 230V unit will burn out on 100V anyway.
- **Towels.** Provided everywhere, including hot springs.
- **More than two pairs of shoes.** You will regret a third pair every time you repack.
- **Heavy winter coat.** A down + shell combination is lighter, packs smaller, and handles a wider temperature range.
- **Jeans.** Slow to dry, heavy, take forever in a coin laundry.
- **A second camera body** unless you're shooting professionally. Phone covers the gaps.
- **Tip envelopes / cash gifts for hosts.** Tipping is not done. See practical-hakone.md.
- **Sake-carrying bottle protectors, hip flasks, or any drinking kit.** Off the list entirely.
