# Practical — Kyoto

## Getting around Kyoto

**Buses vs subway.** Kyoto's tourist-temple geography (Arashiyama west, Kiyomizu east, Kinkaku-ji north, Fushimi Inari south) is poorly served by the two subway lines. **Buses are the workhorse.** But buses are also slow, crowded in autumn, and a meaningful share of your Kyoto patience will be spent on them. Key principles:

- **ICOCA works on city buses, Kyoto Bus, and trains.** Tap on entering Kyoto Bus #17 to Ohara (pay-as-you-enter is standard outside the central flat-rate zone). Tap-on / tap-off on city buses where applicable. Same card works on JR, Hankyu, Keihan, the subway, and konbini.
- **Skip the bus pass.** The Kyoto one-day bus pass (¥700) only pays off if you take 4+ rides; for this itinerary you mostly use buses on Day 12 (Ohara) and Day 14 (Saiho-ji from Arashiyama). ICOCA is cleaner.
- **Subway is fastest for cross-town moves** — Karasuma Line (north-south) and Tozai Line (east-west, runs through Higashiyama). Use it when you can. ¥220–360 per ride.
- **Walking inside Higashiyama is faster than the bus** — Yasaka → Kiyomizu via Sannenzaka is 12 min on foot, 25 min by bus including waits.
- **Taxis** are reasonable (¥500 starter, ¥80/250m), useful for Saiho-ji's awkward access and pre-dawn Fushimi Inari runs from central hotels. JapanTaxi or DiDi apps work.

## Saiho-ji (Kokedera) postcard reservation — step by step

**Mail your postcard in early September 2026** (target a postmark between **early Sep and early Oct** for a Nov 5 visit — the temple accepts applications **2 months to 7 days before** your visit).

The reservation is **mandatory**. Walk-ins are turned away. The reservation system has not changed in 2025/2026 — the temple does have an online portal at *intosaihoji.com* but the postcard method remains the canonical route and is more reliable for visit-date control from abroad.

**What to send:**
1. **An ōfuku-hagaki (return postcard pair) OR a regular postcard + self-addressed return envelope with International Reply Coupons (IRCs) for return postage.** IRCs are sold at Estonian post offices (Omniva) — buy 2–3 to be safe. The return envelope must have *your home address* in Tallinn (or wherever you'd like the confirmation to go — if you'll already be in Japan, address it to your first Japan hotel and contact them in advance).
2. **On the front of the outbound postcard:**
   - To: 〒615-8286 京都市西京区松尾神ヶ谷町56 西芳寺
   - In Roman: Saiho-ji Temple, 56 Matsuo Jingatanicho, Nishikyo-ku, Kyoto 615-8286, Japan
3. **On the back of the outbound postcard, write (English is fine):**
   - Your full name (group representative)
   - Home address (group representative)
   - Phone / email
   - Number of visitors (2)
   - **Preferred visit date** + **2 alternative dates** (e.g. "1st choice: November 5, 2026; 2nd: November 4, 2026; 3rd: November 6, 2026")
   - Names of all visitors
   - A polite request ("I would like to make a reservation to visit Saiho-ji on the dates above.")
4. The return postcard / envelope must already be addressed back to you with return postage attached (IRC if from abroad, or a Japanese stamp ¥85 if you're posting from inside Japan after arrival).

**Cost:** **¥4,000+ per person**, paid in cash at the temple gate on arrival. (Higher fees apply to seasonal special programs.) You must be **13+**.

**What you're booking:** A timed entry to a roughly 60–90 minute program — sutra copying (*shakyo*) on tatami in the main hall, followed by self-paced walk of the moss garden. This is not a museum ticket; it's a temple service.

**Bring on the day:** the confirmation postcard from the temple, ¥4,000+ cash per person, no tripod, no flash. Arrive 10–15 min before your slot; later than 10 min late and you forfeit.

**Verification (as of May 2026):** Postal reservation address and fee structure verified against the temple's official site (saihoji-kokedera.com/postcard/en/) and intosaihoji.com. The online English booking (intosaihoji.com/en/nichinichi/online/) is operational but tends to fill faster during foliage season; the postcard method preserved more date choice for autumn 2025 according to recent traveler reports. **If you can mail by early September**, do the postcard method.

## Haruka to KIX — logistics

- **Schedule:** Every 30 min, ~05:30 first / ~22:30 last from Kyoto. Journey time **75 min**.
- **Fare:** **¥3,440** standard (¥1,910 base fare + ¥1,530 Limited Express reserved seat). Unreserved car is ¥260 cheaper.
- **Tourist discount:** **Haruka One-Way Ticket ¥2,200** — buy at the Midori-no-Madoguchi (JR ticket office) with passport. Cheaper than standard. Sometimes available day-of, sometimes requires online pre-purchase via JR-West Online Reservation. Verify Day 14.
- **Old ICOCA & HARUKA package** (~¥3,200) — **discontinued September 2023.** Don't search for it.
- **Reserved vs unreserved:** Reserve on a Friday or holiday. Unreserved fine off-peak.
- **Luggage:** Trains have an oversized-luggage area at car ends; **must be reserved** in advance for bags >160 cm total dimensions. For two regular suitcases between you, the regular overhead/end-of-car space is enough — but if you forwarded bags ahead, you're traveling carry-on light and this is moot.
- **Where to board:** Platform 30, JR Kyoto Station. Allow 15 min to walk from the central concourse.
- **Arriving KIX:** Train terminates at the airport, walk into Terminal 1 (or take the free shuttle to T2). Pick up forwarded bags at the **JAL ABC / Yamato KIX baggage center** in Terminal 1 ground floor before check-in. Allow at least **2 hours** for international flight check-in + security after Haruka arrival.

## Cash, cards, and konbini

- Kyoto is more card-friendly than Takayama but **Saiho-ji, smaller temples, and machiya stays often want cash**. Carry ¥30,000–40,000 cash.
- **7-Eleven ATMs** in konbini accept Estonian / EU cards. There's one in nearly every block of central Kyoto.
- **Tax-free shopping** at Aritsugu, Yojiya, and most depachika stalls — bring your passport.

## Foliage reality check for Nov 2–6, 2026

- Ohara, Kurama, Sanzen-in: **starting → early color**. Nov 3 timing is good — first quarter of the peak window.
- Tofuku-ji, Eikan-do, Kiyomizu: **mostly green to first tips of yellow/orange.** Peak is Nov 20–30. Frame visits around architecture and garden design, not maple spectacle.
- Arashiyama bamboo grove: always green. The Tenryu-ji garden has some early color.
- Higashiyama / Philosopher's Path: scattered early color, not peak.
- 2025 was a notably late season (warm October); 2026 forecast (per JMA preliminary) suggests a *normal* timeline — meaning don't expect 2025-style late-November pushback into your dates.

## Gion etiquette (current rules)

- **Hanami-koji main street: open to all** during the day and evening.
- **Side alleys marked with "private road" signs: prohibited to tourists, ¥10,000 fine.** Signs are in English, Japanese, Chinese. Stay on the main streets unless you have a confirmed restaurant reservation in a side alley.
- **Do not photograph maiko / geiko without consent.** Don't follow, don't block, don't reach to touch the kimono. Most are commuting between appointments and are working.
- The **Shirakawa Canal** half of Gion (north of Shijo) is less restricted, less crowded, and equally lovely.

## A Nara day-trip note

Your 5 Kyoto nights deliberately *exclude* a Nara day trip. Nara would slot reasonably on Day 12 (Culture Day) — but Nara on Culture Day is also a national meltdown, and Ohara is the better escape. Nara on Day 14 conflicts with Saiho-ji. The skeleton holds.

If you genuinely want Nara, the cleanest swap is: drop **Day 13's Tofuku-ji** (foliage isn't peak anyway) and substitute a **Nara half-day** (Todai-ji + Kasuga Taisha + deer park, JR Nara Line 45 min each way). Day 13 would then be: Fushimi Inari pre-dawn → Nara 11:00–16:00 → back to Gion for evening. Workable. The trade is: lose a Kyoto-depth temple (Tofuku-ji), gain a different-city change of pace and the deer experience for Sonja. Flagging as an optional, not a recommendation.
